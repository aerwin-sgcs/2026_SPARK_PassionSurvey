Phase 4 · 5 minutes

Ignite

Turn one spark into a concrete first step before you leave the room.

Why this matters

A feeling that doesn't become an action fades within days. Small, specific, low-cost commitments made out loud are far more likely to be followed through than general intentions kept private. This phase creates exactly that kind of commitment.

1
Name your spark specifically 1 min

Write one sentence in the box below. Be specific — your teacher will likely need to model this first.

"At the top of your paper, write: 'I think I'm curious about ________ because ________.' Fill in both blanks. Not 'science' — be more specific. Not 'I like it' — tell us why. You've got sixty seconds."
Strong example
"I think I'm curious about how the brain stores memories because I forget things and want to understand why."
Too vague
"I think I'm curious about maths."
2
Choose your micro-action 1 min

Pick one thing to do in the next 48 hours. Maximum 15 minutes. This is not a project — it is a tiny first step.

"You're going to pick one thing to do in the next 48 hours — not a project, not a goal, just a tiny first step."
Your choice:
3
Say it out loud 2 min

When your teacher asks, say three things in ten seconds: your name, your spark, and your action. The point is not accountability — it is hearing yourself say it.

"We're going to go around the room. Each person says three things: your name, your spark, and your action. Ten seconds each. I'll start: I'm curious about how gifted students lose interest in learning, and I'm going to read one paper about it tonight."
Do not explain why you picked your action. Just state it. Justification pressure causes you to second-guess and edit down to something that sounds more impressive — that defeats the purpose.
4
Close the loop 1 min
"Come back and tell me what happened — whether the feeling held up, got bigger, or completely changed. All three are useful information. Finding out you don't actually care about something after trying it? That's not failure, that's exactly what this lesson is for."
